### Attack summary

Card skimming is a form of payment fraud where criminals install hidden hardware devices on card readers at gas stations, ATMs, and retail point-of-sale terminals. The devices capture card magnetic stripe data and PINs from unsuspecting victims. Key characteristics of skimming-based fraud:

**Small probe charges**: Skimmers often make a small test charge ($0–$5) to verify a stolen card is active before selling it or making larger purchases. Cards suddenly showing tiny unfamiliar charges are a strong signal.

**Geographic impossibility**: Skimmed card data is frequently sold and used in a different region or country from where the card was physically present. A card used in New York and then at a gas station in Eastern Europe minutes later is a classic skimming indicator.

**Unusual merchant categories**: Stolen cards are disproportionately used at specific merchant categories: gas stations (easy self-service terminals), grocery stores (high transaction volume, less scrutiny), and online retailers. A card that suddenly transacts at an atypical category (e.g., a card usually used for restaurants suddenly hitting gas stations repeatedly) is suspicious.

**Rapid sequential transactions**: Skimmed cards are used quickly once activated — multiple transactions within a short time window (minutes to hours), often across different merchants, before the card is blocked.

**Spending spike**: A sudden large purchase or cluster of purchases significantly above the cardholder's normal spending average is a strong indicator of fraudulent use after skimming.
